# Modeling gas phase at constant volume - `GAS_PHASE` keyword

## Equilibrium between a solution and gas phase
This example demonstrates equilibration between an aqueous solution, a mineral assemblage, and a constant-volume gas phase. $\mathrm{CO_2(g)}$ is generated by mineral reactions, and gas pressure evolves freely as an equilibrium outcome.
$$

\begin{equation}
\text{CaMg(CO$_{3}$)$_2$(s)} + 2\text{SiO}_2 {\rightleftharpoons} \text{CaMg(Si$_{2}$)O$_6$(s)} + 2\mathrm{CO_2(g)}.
\end{equation}
$$

*Input pressure and partial pressure.* 
In this example the pressure is unknown before the calculation starts. `Pres` keyword is only used as an *initial guess*. Furthermore the partial pressures given in the `GAS_PHASE` as inputs are converted to moles using the ideal gas law. However, the final gas partial pressures are calculated using the Peng Robinson equation of state for mixtures [@appelo2014equations;@ peng1976new].



### Input file

```
GEOCHEM
Temp	350
Pres	300e5

SOLUTION
pH	5 charge
/end

EQUILIBRIUM_PHASES
dolomite 1 0
quartz 1 0
diopside 1 0
/end

GAS_PHASE
volume_frac	1.33
CO2,g	0
H2O,g	0
Ndg,g	10e5
/end
/end
```

*Explanation of input file:*

`GEOCHEM .. /end`:
  :    
  Defines a GEOCHEM calculation block.
`Temp`:
  :    
  temperature of solution, units: Celsius, default $\text{350}^\circ\text{C}$
`Pres`:
  :    
  pressure of solution, units: Pascal. NOTE: `Pres` only serves as an *initial guess* for the numerical solver. In a constant-volume gas phase, pressure is not fixed and is allowed to change during equilibration.
`EQUILIBRIUM_PHASES .. /end`:
  :    
  minerals present, here dolomite, quartz and diopside, see more description in the [solution example](../../solution/markdown/main_solution.md).  
`GAS_PHASE ../end`:
  :    
  * `volume_frac`: ratio of gas volume to water volume
  * $\mathrm{CO_2(g)}$ and $\mathrm{H_2O(g)}$ start at zero, any $\mathrm{CO_2(g)}$ or $\mathrm{H_2O(g)}$ is generated by equilibration.
  * `Ndg,g` redox decoupled nitrogen gas (see below) at initial partial pressure of 10 bar. 


*Redox decoupled gas.* 
In this example, the gas phase includes an inert species (`Ndg`) that does not participate in aqueous speciation or redox reactions. Conceptually, `Ndg` represents a pressure-carrying gas such as $\mathrm{N_2(g)}$, but without explicit redox coupling to the aqueous system. This is because the basis specie is $\text{NO}_3^{-}$ and by default the equilibrium reaction for $\mathrm{N_2(g)}$ is
$\mathrm{N_2(g)}{\rightleftharpoons} 12\text{H}^+ -6 \text{H}_2\text{O} + 2 \text{NO}_3^- + 10 e^-$. This is not a reasonable modeling assumption as nitrogen is for all practical purposes an inert gas.



### Description of output
The pH of the solution is close to 5, due to
* carbonate reactions associated with dolomite
* Dissolution–precipitation equilibria involving $\mathrm{CO_2}$ and bicarbonate

Major aqueous species
* $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$ and $\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$ Released from partial dolomite breakdown during equilibration.
* $\mathrm{HCO_3^-}$ The primary dissolved carbon species, reflecting equilibration between aqueous carbonate chemistry and the $\mathrm{CO_2}$-rich gas phase.
* $\text{SiO}_2$ Controlled by equilibrium with quartz and diopside.

The final pressure close to 240 bar, and the gas phase composition is in the file `<root_name>_gas_phase.out`

*Key observations:*
1. Water vapor dominates the gas phase. At $\text{350}^\circ\text{C}$, water readily partitions into the gas phase. Because the gas volume is fixed, evaporation of water leads to a large contribution to total pressure. This behavior is expected and physically realistic for high-temperature fluid–gas systems.
2. $\mathrm{CO_2}$ is produced by mineral equilibration. The presence of ~27 mol  $\mathrm{CO_₂}$ reflects production via the buffered reaction in the equation at the top. $\mathrm{CO_2}$ accumulates in the gas phase until its fugacity satisfies the mineral equilibrium, at which point further decarbonation is inhibited.

*Notice.* 
$\mathrm{CO_2}$  pressure is an output, not an imposed condition. Its final value reflects coupling between mineral stability and gas volume



## Appendix: Gas buffers and Peng-Robinson parameters
For a (real) gas the thermodynamic activity is measured relative to an reference state of atmospheric pressure $p_0=1$ atm, e.g. for hydrogen gas
$$
\begin{equation}
a_\mathrm{H_2(g)}=\frac{f_\mathrm{H_2(g)}}{p_0},
\end{equation}
$$
where $f$ is called the *fugacity*. For an ideal gas the fugacity is simply the partial pressure (i.e. the pressure the gas would exert if it was the only gas in a container), for real gasses at a (mechanical) pressure of $p$, the fugacity is different $f=\phi p$. $\phi$ is called a *fugacity coefficient*. The fugacity coefficient are in many cases estimated from [@appelo2014equations] (Note that in the paper the last term in the equation below has a `+` term, this is not correct)
$$
\begin{equation}
\ln \phi=\left(\frac{P V_m}{R T}-1\right)-\ln\left(\frac{P(V_m-b)}{RT}\right)
-\frac{a\alpha(T)}{2.828 b R T}\ln\left(\frac{V_m+2.414 b}{V_m-0.414b}\right),
\end{equation}
$$
where the molar volume, $V_m$, and pressure $P$ is determined from the Peng-Robinson equation of state (EOS)
$$
\begin{equation}
P = \frac{RT}{V_m-b}-\frac{\alpha(T) a}{V_m^2+2bV_m-b^2}.
\end{equation}
$$
The parameters are
$$

a=0.457235 \frac{(RT_c)^2}{p_c}\\ 
b=0.077796\frac{RT_c}{p_c}\\ 
\alpha(T)=\left(1+(0.37464+1.54226\omega -0.26992\omega^2)\left(1-\sqrt{\frac{T}{T_c}}\right)\right)^2	

$$
$p_c$ and $T_c$ are the critical pressure and temperature respectively. Below are the values listed that are already included in `GeoChemX`. Note that the three last entries are *redox decoupled* versions of hydrogen, hydrogen sulfide and nitrogen gas. Wh
