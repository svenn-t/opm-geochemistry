Equilibrium between a solution and gas phase

This example demonstrates equilibration between an aqueous solution, a mineral assemblage, and a constant-volume gas phase. \( \co \) is generated by mineral reactions, and gas pressure evolves freely as an equilibrium outcome.

$$ \begin{equation} \text{CaMg(CO$_{3}$)$_2$(s)} + 2\text{SiO}_2 \cheq \text{CaMg(Si$_{2}$)O$_6$(s)} + 2\co. \tag{1} \end{equation} $$
Input pressure and partial pressure

In this example the pressure is unknown before the calculation starts. Pres keyword is only used as an initial guess. Furthermore the partial pressures given in the GAS_PHASE as inputs are converted to moles using the ideal gas law. However, the final gas partial pressures are calculated using the Peng Robinson equation of state for mixtures [1] [2].

Input file 

GEOCHEM
Temp	350
Pres	300e5

SOLUTION
pH	5 charge
/end

EQUILIBRIUM_PHASES
dolomite 1 0
quartz 1 0
diopside 1 0
/end

GAS_PHASE
volume_frac	1.33
CO2,g	0
H2O,g	0
Ndg,g	10e5
/end
/end

Explanation of input file:

GEOCHEM .. /end:

Defines a GEOCHEM calculation block.

Temp:

temperature of solution, units: Celsius, default \( \text{350}^\circ\text{C} \)

Pres:

pressure of solution, units: Pascal. NOTE: Pres only serves as an initial guess for the numerical solver. In a constant-volume gas phase, pressure is not fixed and is allowed to change during equilibration.

EQUILIBRIUM_PHASES .. /end:

minerals present, here dolomite, quartz and diopside, see more description in the solution example.

GAS_PHASE ../end:
  • volume_frac: ratio of gas volume to water volume
  • \( \co \) and \( \htg \) start at zero, any \( \co \) or \( \htg \) is generated by equilibration.
  • Ndg,g redox decoupled nitrogen gas (see below) at initial partial pressure of 10 bar.
Redox decoupled gas

In this example, the gas phase includes an inert species (Ndg) that does not participate in aqueous speciation or redox reactions. Conceptually, Ndg represents a pressure-carrying gas such as \( \nig \), but without explicit redox coupling to the aqueous system. This is because the basis specie is \( \text{NO}_3^{-} \) and by default the equilibrium reaction for \( \nig \) is \( \nig\cheq 12\text{H}^+ -6 \text{H}_2\text{O} + 2 \text{NO}_3^- + 10 e^- \). This is not a reasonable modeling assumption as nitrogen is for all practical purposes an inert gas.

Description of output

The pH of the solution is close to 5, due to

  • carbonate reactions associated with dolomite
  • Dissolution–precipitation equilibria involving \( \COTW \) and bicarbonate

Major aqueous species

  • \( \Ca \) and \( \Mg \) Released from partial dolomite breakdown during equilibration.
  • \( \HCOT \) The primary dissolved carbon species, reflecting equilibration between aqueous carbonate chemistry and the \( \COTW \)-rich gas phase.
  • \( \text{SiO}_2 \) Controlled by equilibrium with quartz and diopside.

The final pressure close to 240 bar, and the gas phase composition is in the file <root_name>_gas_phase.out

Key observations:

  1. Water vapor dominates the gas phase. At \( \text{350}^\circ\text{C} \), water readily partitions into the gas phase. Because the gas volume is fixed, evaporation of water leads to a large contribution to total pressure. This behavior is expected and physically realistic for high-temperature fluid–gas systems.
  2. \( \COTW \) is produced by mineral equilibration. The presence of ~27 mol \( \mathrm{CO_₂} \) reflects production via the buffered reaction in the equation at the top. \( \COTW \) accumulates in the gas phase until its fugacity satisfies the mineral equilibrium, at which point further decarbonation is inhibited.
Notice

\( \COTW \) pressure is an output, not an imposed condition. Its final value reflects coupling between mineral stability and gas volume

Appendix: Gas buffers and Peng-Robinson parameters

For a (real) gas the thermodynamic activity is measured relative to an reference state of atmospheric pressure \( p_0=1 \) atm, e.g. for hydrogen gas

$$ \begin{equation} a_\hy=\frac{f_\hy}{p_0}, \tag{2} \end{equation} $$

where \( f \) is called the fugacity. For an ideal gas the fugacity is simply the partial pressure (i.e. the pressure the gas would exert if it was the only gas in a container), for real gasses at a (mechanical) pressure of \( p \), the fugacity is different \( f=\phi p \). \( \phi \) is called a fugacity coefficient. The fugacity coefficient are in many cases estimated from [1] (Note that in the paper the last term in the equation below has a + term, this is not correct)

$$ \begin{equation} \ln \phi=\left(\frac{P V_m}{R T}-1\right)-\ln\left(\frac{P(V_m-b)}{RT}\right) -\frac{a\alpha(T)}{2.828 b R T}\ln\left(\frac{V_m+2.414 b}{V_m-0.414b}\right), \tag{3} \end{equation} $$

where the molar volume, \( V_m \), and pressure \( P \) is determined from the Peng-Robinson equation of state (EOS)

$$ \begin{equation} P = \frac{RT}{V_m-b}-\frac{\alpha(T) a}{V_m^2+2bV_m-b^2}. \tag{4} \end{equation} $$

The parameters are

$$ \begin{align} a&=0.457235 \frac{(RT_c)^2}{p_c} \tag{5}\\ b&=0.077796\frac{RT_c}{p_c} \tag{6}\\ \alpha(T)&=\left(1+(0.37464+1.54226\omega -0.26992\omega^2)\left(1-\sqrt{\frac{T}{T_c}}\right)\right)^2 \tag{7} \end{align} $$

\( p_c \) and \( T_c \) are the critical pressure and temperature respectively. Below are the values listed that are already included in GeoChemX. Note that the three last entries are redox decoupled versions of hydrogen, hydrogen sulfide and nitrogen gas. Wh
